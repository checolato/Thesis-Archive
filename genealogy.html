Reflective City (About)

Genealogy

  • Taoyuan Irrigation System
    A canal system shaping settlement patterns, microclimates, agriculture, and daily life. The ponds formed a spatial logic of orientation, labor, memory, and ecology.
  • Hakka Water-Village Settlement Patterns
    Hakka communities in southern China and Taiwan often arranged homes, farmlands, and social spaces around ponds and water courts. Spatial identity emerged from water edges, circulation paths, and agricultural tasks. This reinforces water’s role as the primary organizer of human presence.
  • Vietnamese Mekong Wetland Settlements
    Communities organize homes, markets, and routes around shifting water levels. The spatial logic of stilt houses, floating markets, and seasonal pathways parallels the interest in water-driven movement and environmental familiarity.
  • Indigenous Taiwanese Water-Trail Knowledge
    Atayal and Amis communities use river routes, water rhythms, and seasonal flows as navigation systems.
  • Japanese Satoyama Landscapes
    These human–nature interface landscapes integrate ponds, paddies, forests, and villages as continuous ecological systems. Their spatial practice shows how water bodies guide routes, habits, and seasonal rhythms.
  • Hong Kong Wetland Park
    A designed ecological landscape where visitors learn place identity through humidity, sound, reflection, and biodiversity. It frames water as a multisensory spatial educator, a model for how ponds teach human agents in digital twins.
  • Taiwan’s Urban Wetland Revitalization
    From Taipei’s Guandu to Taoyuan’s Laojie River areas, these projects frame water ecosystems as living archives. They show how restoring wetlands revives community identity, movement, and sensory experience.
  • Hsinchu Pond Parks Network
    An urban network of ponds preserved as ecological corridors and public spaces. Demonstrates how ponds form spatial memory and daily walking circuits for residents.
  • Melbourne Water Sensitive Urban Design
    Projects integrate water bodies into neighborhoods through retention basins, wetlands, and reflective catchments. It shows how water influences microclimate, comfort, and pedestrian behavior.
  • Ang Mo Kio River Park
    A concrete canal transformed into a meandering river with ponds and wetlands. People experience seasonal water rhythms, humidity shifts, and wildlife.
  • Living Hydrological Systems
    Mountain-scale hydrological design integrating water flows with community rhythms, planting cycles, and ecological care. A spatial precedent for “water as timekeeper” and “topography as memory,” relevant to how digital twins tie ponds to human cycles.
  • Rotterdam Water Squares
    Plazas that fill with water during heavy rains, creating seasonal spatial transformations. Water becomes a time-based agent shaping social use and memory.
  • Smellmapping
    Creates urban maps based on scent walks.
  • Soundwalks
    Soundwalks frame cities as acoustic fields learned through attentive listening. Pond ecologies (frogs, insects, water movement) fit naturally into this lineage as auditory landmarks for familiarity.
  • Glowworm Cave Tourism
    Visitors learn orientation in near-total darkness guided only by water-reflection, moisture, and sound.
  • Reflective Water Installations
    Works where water surfaces create shifting perception, orientation, and memory.
  • Light Spatial Installations
    Creates spaces where environmental or invisible data becomes sensory experience.
  • Meteorological Architecture
    Designs architectures organized by humidity, temperature, airflow.
  • Stalker / Osservatorio Nomade
    Collective walking projects mapping the “actual territories” of Rome’s edges: abandoned, wet, rough, unplanned. Their method of embodied fieldwork parallels how to treat ponds as sensory field generators shaping human traces.
