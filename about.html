Reflective City

Reconstructing Place Familiarity in Taoyuan with a Human-Centric Digital Twin for Sensory Architecture

Research Question

How can memory become a form of orientation when the tectonic city is no longer legible?

Abstract

This thesis begins with a feeling familiar to many who grew up in rapidly developing cities: the disorientation of returning home and realizing that the places that shaped us have vanished. As skylines rise and districts are rebuilt, the environmental cues that once grounded our memories dissolve, creating a widening gap between the physical city and the one we carry inside us. This gap raises a deeper question: what happens to memory when the landscape that held it disappears?

Taoyuan, Taiwan, a city defined by industrial parks, logistics hubs, and its proximity to Taipei, has undergone one of the fastest transformations in the country. With the launch of the Taoyuan Aerotropolis plan, thousands of hectares were rezoned for airport-centered development. Entire neighborhoods were cleared; farmland and family homes were absorbed into an emerging financial district. In every sweep of excavation, soil covered not only agricultural land but also the stories, rhythms, and futures of the people who lived there.

Amid these shifts, this project turns to one of Taoyuan’s most characteristic landscapes: the 埤塘 (pond). More than remnants of an agricultural past, these ponds act as sensory anchors—sites of humidity, reflection, sound, and ecological life that once imprinted themselves onto daily experience. They serve as emotional and mnemonic cues that endure even when the built environment does not.

This thesis proposes a Human-Centered Digital Twin (H-DT) that translates pond-based sensory logics into interactive systems for present-day residents. Instead of treating the pond as a vanished landscape, the twin treats its hydrological behaviors—humidity gradients, reflective light, and ecological sound—as active mediators of human presence and spatial perception. These sensory cues become data-driven conditions that influence how human agents learn, navigate, and annotate urban space.

By engaging with the digital twin, residents can participate in reconstructing the affective and ecological qualities that once grounded Taoyuan, even if the original ponds no longer exist. Through this approach, the digital twin becomes a platform for co-creating new forms of familiarity within a city that continues to shift beneath its inhabitants.
